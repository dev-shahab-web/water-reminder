# User Personas

## Primary Persona: Busy Desk Worker

### Profile

Works at a desk, often forgets to drink water during focused work, wants gentle reminders without a complex wellness routine.

### Goals

- Build a steady hydration habit.
- Log drinks quickly between tasks.
- Avoid disruptive notifications during meetings or deep work.

### Frustrations

- Reminder apps can become noisy.
- Tracking apps often require too many taps.
- Wellness apps may feel judgmental.

### Product Needs

- Simple home progress.
- Quiet-hour and interval control.
- Fast quick-add actions.
- Clear daily completion feedback.

## Secondary Persona: Active Lifestyle User

### Profile

Exercises regularly, may need more water on active days, and likes lightweight metrics.

### Goals

- Adjust daily goal depending on activity.
- Review history and progress.
- Log larger amounts after workouts.

### Frustrations

- Fixed reminders do not account for active days.
- Generic bottle sizes may not match personal bottles.

### Product Needs

- Editable goal.
- Custom quick-add sizes.
- Weekly trend view in a later release.

## Secondary Persona: Habit Beginner

### Profile

Wants to improve health habits but does not know what daily water goal to choose.

### Goals

- Start without research.
- Receive encouragement.
- Avoid guilt after missing days.

### Frustrations

- Too much configuration causes abandonment.
- Streak pressure can feel discouraging.

### Product Needs

- Recommended default goal.
- Skip-friendly onboarding.
- Supportive, neutral copy.

## Anti-Personas

- Users seeking medical hydration management.
- Users who need clinician-managed care plans.
- Users who want calorie, macro, or diet coaching as the main experience.
- Teams or employers monitoring employee hydration.

## Assumptions

- Most early users want utility, not a social wellness network.
- Daily hydration is a personal habit with high variance by climate, body, diet, activity, and medical context.
- Retention depends more on reminder quality and logging speed than on visual complexity.

## Edge Cases

- Night-shift users may define a "day" differently.
- Users may fast for religious or health reasons and need temporary reminder suppression.
- Users with accessibility needs may rely entirely on voiceover or large text.
- Users may track for someone else, such as a child or older relative, even if not an explicit persona.

## Suggested Improvements

- Add a "schedule profile" concept for workdays, weekends, and travel.
- Add gentle copy modes: neutral, encouraging, or minimal.
- Add a future caretaker mode only if validated by user research.

## Trade-Offs

- Designing for the busy desk worker keeps MVP focused, but may under-serve athletes.
- Avoiding medical use reduces risk, but requires careful copy around recommendations.
- Supporting night-shift schedules improves inclusivity, but complicates daily boundaries and reminders.
